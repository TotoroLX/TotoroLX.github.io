KAM theory for properly-degenerate system

Hamiltonian function is of the following form $$ H(I, \varphi, p, q; \mu) := H_0(I) + \mu f(I, \varphi, p, q) $$ whose phase space is \begin{equation} \mathcal{P} := V \times \mathbb{T}^{n_1} \times B^{2n_2} \end{equation} where \( V \) is an open, bounded and connected subset of \( \mathbb{R}^{n_1}\), and \( (I, \varphi) \in V \times \mathbb{T}^{n_1}\), \(B^{2n_2} \subset \mathbb{R}^{2n_2} \) is a ball around the origin, and \( (p,q) \in B^{2n_2} \).

Some assumptions should be satisfied (the averaged perturbation \( f_{av} \) is in Birkhoff normal form of order \( 2s \)).

To prove the main theorem, we first (Step 1) apply the average theory, which gives us a transformation $$ \phi_{\alpha}: (I_{\alpha}, \varphi_{\alpha}, p_{\alpha}, q_{\alpha}) \rightarrow (I, \varphi, p, q) $$ and results in $$ H_{\alpha} := H \circ \phi_{\alpha} \ = H_0(I_{\alpha}) + \mu g_{\alpha}(I_{\alpha}, p_{\alpha}, q_{\alpha}) + \mu f_{\alpha}(I_{\alpha}, \varphi_{\alpha}, p_{\alpha}, q_{\alpha}) $$

The average theory tells us that the transformation \(\phi_{\alpha}\) is close to identity, \(g_{\alpha}\) is close to \(f_{av}\), and \(f_{\alpha}\) is of order \(O(\epsilon^{2s+1})\).
